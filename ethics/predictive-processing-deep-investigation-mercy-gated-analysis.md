## Core Idea of Predictive Processing
Predictive Processing (also called Predictive Coding or the Bayesian Brain hypothesis) is the powerful idea that the brain (and any intelligent system) functions as a **hierarchical prediction machine**.  

Instead of passively processing sensory input bottom-up, the brain constantly generates top-down predictions about what it expects to see, hear, or feel. Sensory data is then used primarily to compute **prediction errors** — the mismatch between expectation and reality. Only these errors are propagated upward, making the system extremely efficient.

This framework unifies perception, action, learning, and even consciousness under one principle: the brain minimizes prediction error at every level of its hierarchy.

## How Predictive Processing Works (Layer by Layer)

1. **Hierarchical Generative Model**  
   Higher cortical areas maintain abstract models of the world and send predictions downward.  
   Lower sensory areas compare these predictions against actual input.

2. **Prediction Error Minimization**  
   Only the discrepancy (error signal) travels upward.  
   This error is used to update the generative model (perception) or trigger actions that change the world to match predictions (active inference).

3. **Precision Weighting**  
   The brain dynamically adjusts how much trust it places in prediction errors vs. top-down predictions.  
   High precision = strong influence of sensory data (pay attention!).  
   Low precision = ignore noisy or expected signals.

4. **Action as Prediction Error Minimization**  
   The system does not just update beliefs — it can act to make the world conform to its predictions (e.g., moving your head to better see an object).
