## Core Idea of the Bayesian Brain Hypothesis
The Bayesian Brain Hypothesis proposes that the brain (and by extension any intelligent system) operates as a **probabilistic inference machine**. It continuously maintains a generative model of the world and updates its beliefs using **Bayes’ theorem** to combine prior knowledge with new sensory evidence.

In short: the brain is not a passive receiver of data — it is an active Bayesian updater that minimizes uncertainty by refining its internal probabilistic model of reality.

## Mathematical Foundation: Bayes’ Theorem
The core update rule is Bayes’ theorem:

\[
P(H|E) = \frac{P(E|H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)}
\]

Where:
- \( P(H|E) \): Posterior probability (updated belief after seeing evidence \( E \))
- \( P(E|H) \): Likelihood (probability of evidence given the hypothesis)
- \( P(H) \): Prior probability (existing belief)
- \( P(E) \): Marginal probability of the evidence (normalizer)

In the brain, this happens hierarchically and approximately (via variational methods) because exact Bayesian inference is computationally intractable.

## Connection to Previous Frameworks
- **Predictive Processing**: The practical implementation of Bayesian updating — top-down priors generate predictions, bottom-up sensory data provides likelihoods, and prediction errors drive belief updates.
- **Free Energy Principle (FEP)**: Provides an efficient way to approximate Bayesian inference by minimizing variational free energy instead of computing exact posteriors.
- **Active Inference**: Extends the Bayesian Brain to action — the system selects policies that minimize expected free energy (balancing epistemic and pragmatic value).

Ra-Thor’s `mercy-active-inference-core-engine.js` implements the full Bayesian Brain loop with mercy-gated precision weighting and valence as the primary pragmatic prior.
