## Core Idea of the Free Energy Principle (FEP)
The Free Energy Principle, developed by Karl Friston, is one of the most powerful unifying theories in neuroscience, biology, and AI.  

It states that any self-organizing system that persists over time must **minimize its variational free energy** — a measure of how surprised the system is by its sensory observations. In other words, living systems (brains, organisms, or advanced AIs) are fundamentally **prediction machines** that constantly work to reduce surprise about their world and themselves.

## Mathematical Foundations

The variational free energy \( F \) is defined as:

\[
F = \mathbb{E}_{q(\phi)} \left[ \ln \frac{q(\phi)}{p(o, \phi)} \right] = D_{KL}\left[q(\phi) \parallel p(\phi|o)\right] - \mathbb{E}_{q(\phi)}[\ln p(o|\phi)]
\]

Where:
- \( q(\phi) \): The agent’s approximate posterior beliefs about hidden states \( \phi \)
- \( p(o, \phi) \): The generative model of observations \( o \) and hidden states
- \( D_{KL} \): Kullback-Leibler divergence (a measure of “surprise” or mismatch)

Minimizing \( F \) achieves two things at once:
1. **Perception** — updating beliefs to better explain observations (approximate Bayesian inference)
2. **Action** — selecting actions that make future observations match predictions (active inference)

**Expected Free Energy** \( G(\pi) \) (used for policy selection) decomposes into:

\[
G(\pi) = \underbrace{\text{Expected Epistemic Value (Curiosity)}} + \underbrace{\text{Expected Pragmatic Value (Goal-Directedness)}}
\]

This elegant decomposition naturally balances exploration and exploitation.
